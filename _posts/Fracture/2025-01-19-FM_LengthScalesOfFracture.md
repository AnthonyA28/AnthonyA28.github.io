Previously we discussed the critical energy release rate $\Gamma$. Recall that Griffith  predicted the following:
$$
\Gamma = 2\gamma
$$
Where $\gamma$ is the surface energy of the material. That is, the critical energy release rate is equal to the energy required to create two new surfaces. The experiments which validated Griffith's hypothesis were done on glass and indeed verified the hypothesis. There are virtually no other materials that follow this prediction. Although, the prediction fails, the energetic approach of Griffith is valid and provides insight as to what underlies fracture in other materials. 

# The Elasto-Adhesive Length Scale



Starting with the two basic material properties of materials, we can do basic non-dimensional analysis to arrive at a length scale. Consider the units of $\Gamma [=] \frac{J}{m^2}$ and the units of the elastic modulus $E [=] \frac{N}{m^2} = \frac{J}{m^3}$ . Combining these in a rational ways results in :
$$
L_{EA} = \frac{\Gamma}{E} 
$$
 Which is the **elasto-adhesive length**. This ratio is a comparison of energy spent to open cracks to the energy density from deformation. It represents the length around the crack which will undergo large deformations - large enough to dissipate plastic or viscous energy. When the elasto-adhesive length is larger that characteristic specimen sizes (i.e. crack length or sample thickness or length), the energy balance of Griffith will no longer capture the energy spent on growing cracks, but the energy spent, dissipated, on deforming the specimen. 

### The elasto-adhesive length of conventional glass

Consider the basic calculation for glass (using some representative values):
$$
L_{EA} = \frac{10 \frac{J}{m^2}}{50\cdot 10^{9} \frac{N}{m^2}} = 2\cdot 10^{-10} m
$$
which is two angstroms. This is about the length of a chemical bond! Thus,  bulk energy dissipation need not be considered when fracturing conventional glasses. Although, glasses can be designed to increase this length to make them tougher. 



### The elasto-adhesive length for a typical soft adhesive

Soft adhesives have elastic moduli which can vary significantly with rate. For example, it can be as low as 0.1MPa to 100MPa. The toughness can also range from 10$\frac{J}{m^2}$ to 10,000$\frac{J}{m^2}$. The resulting elasto-adhesive length can vary from 100 microns to upwards of a 1mm. depending on the rate. That means the region of energy dissipation can extend through the entire thickness of the adhesive. Therefore, the relevant length scale to consider when modelling fracture of adhesives, is not the crack length or dissipation length, but the thickness of the adhesive.
